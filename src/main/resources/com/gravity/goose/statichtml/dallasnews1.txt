<div class="art-story__content-wrapper">
    <div class="art-story__text-wrapper row-m row-l row-xl">
        <div class="art-story__text">
            <p><i>Richard Rothstein's new book, "The Color of Law, A Forgotten History of How Our Government Segregated America," explains how, for more than 100 years, the U.S. government practiced, enforced and allowed segregation in housing. Rothstein makes a powerful case that, despite the 1968 Fair Housing Act, the public policies of yesterday still shape the racial landscape of today.&nbsp;</i></p><p><strong>Why is current-day America so ignorant of how pervasive and overt the government's official role has been in creating and continuing housing segregation?</strong></p><p>Because it's so difficult to remedy, we've chosen, collectively as a people, to wish the problem away by claiming that it happened by accident, and therefore it's not something that requires a constitutional remedy.</p><p><strong>By accident?</strong></p><p>The standard consensus, articulated by the Supreme Court and adopted across the political spectrum, is that neighborhoods are segregated de facto, which means by personal choices of where to live, where private prejudice, private discrimination, or private real estate agents steer families  by accident  but not government action. Rather than acknowledging it as a consistent conscious policy, it's wrongly been seen as just a million personal decisions resulting in the concentration of African-Americans in segregated neighborhoods. So it's very difficult to think how to bring about a million opposite personal decisions to desegregate.</p>        </div>
    </div>
</div><div class="art-story__content-wrapper ">
    <figure class="art-story__embed art-story__embed--first-party-dmn">
        <aside role="complementary" class="dmn-embed" data-embed-type="collection" data-embed="https://www.dallasnews.com/embed/story/points-summer-book-club-2017-evicted" lang="en"><a href="https://www.dallasnews.com/opinion/commentary/collection/points-summer-book-club-2017-evicted">Points Summer Book Club 2017</a></aside><div class="lazyload" data-script="https://www.dallasnews.com/resources/embed/embed.js" data-expand="200"></div>    </figure>
</div>
<div class="art-story__content-wrapper">
    <div class="art-story__text-wrapper row-m row-l row-xl">
        <div class="art-story__text">
            <p>That's contrasted with what actually has been the case, de jure segregation, meaning segregation by racially explicit government law and policy. If we have de jure segregation, it's a constitutional violation and thus requires a constitutional remedy. As I said, since that remedy is so difficult to imagine, we would rather avoid understanding that we're required to undertake it.</p><p><strong>How about an example of de jure segregation?</strong></p><p>The FHA's [Federal Housing Administration] policy of financing entire suburbs on condition that no homes be sold to African-Americans, and that indeed, the homes in those suburbs have deed clauses prohibiting resale to African-Americans. I have received many, many emails from people who lived in, for example, Levittown in New York or other suburbs like that whose parents moved there after World War II and who said "there were no African-Americans, but it never occurred to me why that was the case."</p><p>Those homes were so modestly priced that African-Americans as well as whites could've purchased them easily. For instance, the FHA subsidized the suburbs that were built after World War II in the early 1950s and those houses sold for $8,000. African-Americans could've afforded those as well as whites. But through this policy, the FHA suburbanized the country, establishing entire suburbs on a racially exclusive basis.</p><p><strong>I learned so much about the history of housing policy from your book. From your speaking engagements, what do you find surprises readers the most?</strong></p><p>That segregated housing was a consistent nationwide policy  law in the North and the Midwest. It's very comfortable for people that segregation is a Southern problem. But because schools, buses and restaurants were not segregated in the North, I think people assumed that residential segregation in the North must not be an official government policy.</p><p>Also, when I tell stories of how public housing was used to segregate communities that hadn't known segregation before.</p><p><strong>You write about an example along those lines that took place down the road from us, in Austin.</strong></p><p>In Austin, as well as throughout the South, there were many integrated neighborhoods. That was because working-class families didn't have automobiles, so they had to be able to walk to work or take very short public transportation rides. If you had industrial facilities, with black and Polish immigrant or Irish immigrant or Italian immigrant or rural white workers all working together, they had to live in the same neighborhoods in order to be able to get to work.</p><p>That was the case in Austin until they developed a city plan in 1928 in which they designated the east side as a black neighborhood and began a series of programs to relocate African-Americans who lived throughout the city to that area.</p><p>As a new congressman [less than a decade later] Lyndon Johnson was an aggressive promoter of public housing as a New Deal program. And the first three projects were established in Austin. One for African-Americans, in the east side area, one for Hispanics, not far from the east side, and one for whites, farther away.</p><p>The city then began to take aggressive steps to move all blacks who were living elsewhere into the east side neighborhood.</p><p>They did not do that for Hispanics, so although there was a segregated Hispanic project, there was no effort to move all Hispanics in the city out of the neighborhoods where they lived and into the area around this Hispanic project. So the segregation of African-Americans was much, much more aggressive than for Hispanics.</p><p><strong>Beyond setting the record straight on the government's real role in housing segregation, what impact would you like to see your book have?</strong></p><p>My hope is that it will contribute to a new national consensus that de facto segregation is a myth and rather that our neighborhoods nationwide were segregated by government policy in an unconstitutional fashion. And that we'll begin to have a conversation on how to remedy it instead of accepting as an unfortunate, inevitable result of millions of private decisions</p><p>We're not prepared today to engage in that conversation about how to remedy it because we don't have a consensus that it was state-sponsored.</p><p>People say, "You should send this book to every congressman." Well, congressmen are not the people who need it. The people who need it are the constituents to whom Congress reports because even if Congress wanted to reform this, there would not be public support to do it.</p><p><strong>Do you get any sense that toeholds are developing for that conversation?</strong></p><p>When I first started writing this book, almost 10 years ago, it was inconceivable that we could have the kind of national conversations about race that we're having. After Michael Brown was killed in Ferguson, Missouri, we began to have the beginnings of a national conversation about race, and many, many hopeful signs continue.</p><p>For example, the confrontation over the glorification of the Confederacy throughout the South. Mayor Mitch Landrieu's speech in New Orleans when the Confederate statues were removed was very moving and something that I think five years ago was inconceivable for a Southern white politician.</p>        </div>

    <div class="art-story__text-wrapper row-m row-l row-xl">
        <div class="art-story__text">
            <p>So, I am hopeful that we are beginning this conversation and that it'll become more sophisticated and more widespread as it continues.</p><p>The book has been adopted by book clubs in many places, by ordinary people. That's where the conversations should begin.</p><p>I'm devoting the next year to almost non-stop lecturing and speaking about these issues, as much as possible in so-called red states, events in places like Kansas City and Wichita, because it's too easy to just talk about this only where you get enthusiastic receptions.</p><p><strong>What's been the impact on education as a result of long-held segregated housing policy?</strong></p><p>The reason we can't achieve what we are hoping to achieve in education, which is sometimes referred to as closing the black-white achievement gap, it's because when you have children who have serious economic and social problems, who come to school either poorly nourished or in ill health, they've been up at night, they are under great stress because of parental unemployment.</p><p>If you have a few children in the school like that, teachers can devote special attention to them. If you have schools in which every child has some or several of those problems, the schools are overwhelmed by those problems and resources have to be devoted to remediation rather than on grade-level instruction.</p><p>It's inconceivable that we can close the black-white achievement gap so long as we segregate the most disadvantaged children in this country in single schools, and we can't desegregate those schools unless we desegregated the neighborhoods in which they're located.</p><p><strong>Throughout the book are examples of the government thwarting long-term integration gains.</strong></p><p>Yes, for instance, the 1949 Housing Act, which conservatives tried to saddle with an amendment prohibiting segregation and racial discrimination in public housing, knowing that Southern Democrats would kill the legislation.</p><p>Liberals had to fight the amendment in order to get <em>any</em> public housing program. And Paul Douglas, who was a leading liberal in the Senate, concluded his peroration to try to persuade his fellow liberals to vote against integration  which he did successfully  by saying his "Negro friends" would be much better off with the housing provided on a segregated basis than they would be with no housing at all.</p><p>I'm not sure he was right. I think they would've been better off with no housing at all than with the segregated public housing that solidified their segregated and disadvantaged status for the next 50 years.</p><p><em>This Q&amp;A was conducted, edited and condensed by Dallas Morning News editorial writer Sharon Grigsby. Email:</em> <a href="mailto:sgrigsby@dallasnews.com"><em>sgrigsby@dallasnews.com</em></a><em>.</em></p><p><em style="font-size: 1em;">Richard Rothstein is a research associate at the Economic Policy Institute. Email: epi@epi.org.</em><br></p><h3 id="whatsyourview" class="align--center">What's your view?</h3><p>Got an opinion about this issue?&nbsp;<a href="http://www.dallasnews.com/opinion/letters-to-the-editor/2017/02/09/submit-letter-editor" style="font-size: 1em;">Send a letter to the editor</a>, and you just might get published.</p>        </div>
