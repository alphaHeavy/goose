/ Kate Hiscock</a></span></span></figure></p><h3> About the jargon </h3><p><strong>A security is a financial instrument.</strong> You'll probably hear people refer to "securities," which is a catch-all term for things like <a href=http://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/security.asp >stocks, bonds, or CDs</a>. Securities are divided into debt securities (money owed to us, like from a government bond), and equity securities (actual value we own, like stocks). </p><p><strong>Stocks are equity in a company.</strong> When you buy a stock, you're buying a tiny little piece of an actual company. Not <em>a lot</em>, but ownership nonetheless. Stocks are more volatile than bonds, and may therefore yield greater rewards or losses.

The major stock exchanges in the US include the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) and the Nasdaq. Stock prices are also tracked on indices such as the S&P 500 and the Dow Jones Industrial Average. While you'll want to check in with your individual investments, monitoring stock market activity can give you an idea of how your portfolio might be performing. </p><p><strong>Bonds are loans you make.</strong> When you purchase a bond, you're essentially loaning a little money to an entity &mdash; like the US government, for instance &mdash; and that entity has to pay you back after a fixed period of time, with interest. There aren't bond exchanges that show up in a ticker, because bonds are traded differently than stocks. However, there are sites where you can get an idea of bond pricing, like the <a href=http://online.wsj.com/mdc/public/page/2_3022-bondbnchmrk.html target=_blank>Wall Street Journal</a>. </p><p><strong>Diversification means spreading your money out among different kinds of investments.</strong> There are a lot of opinions out there about <a href=http://www.businessinsider.com/what-people-get-wrong-on-diversification-2014-2 target=_blank>how diversified an investment portfolio needs to be</a>, but most everyone agrees that putting all of your financial eggs in one basket is a recipe for disaster. </p><p><strong>The ROI is how much money you make on your investments.</strong> To get an idea of how well your investments are performing, you can <a href=http://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/returnoninvestment.asp target=_blank>calculate the ROI</a> by dividing an investment's gains by its costs. </p><p><figure id="img-253013"                                     </span></span></figure></p><h3> About the process </h3><p><strong>You'll probably be charged fees.</strong> Investing isn't free. If you're working with an investment professional, you'll pay them either a percentage of your portfolio or a flat fee (you'll want to know if your advisor is <a href=http://www.businessinsider.com/fee-only-versus-fee-based-financial-advisors-2014-7 target=_blank>"fee-based" or "fee-only"</a> before you sign on), <a href=http://www.businessinsider.com/robo-advisors-vs-financial-advisors-2014-7 > online investment platforms o</a><a href=http://www.businessinsider.com/robo-advisors-vs-financial-advisors-2014-7 > r "robo-advisors"</a> each have their own fee structures, and some <a href=http://www.businessinsider.com/5-charts-to-put-mutual-fund-expenses-in-perspective-2014-7 > mutual funds and ETFs also charge fees</a>. These fees vary, and if you do your research, you can minimize them.

When you cash in, you'll owe what's called <a href=http://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/capital_gains_tax.asp >capital gains taxes</a>. </p><p><strong>... but you also may receive a tax break.</strong> Although different retirement accounts have different tax structures, contributions are often tax-deductible. <a href=http://www.businessinsider.com/how-to-use-a-529-college-savings-plan-2014-4 target=_blank>529 savings plans</a>, which are also investment accounts, are similarly tax-advantaged. </p><p><strong>Sometimes, you'll fail.</strong> It's an unfortunate truth that we won't all be rock star investors. For some people to do really well, <a href=http://www.businessinsider.com/some-investors-are-bound-to-fail-2014-5 > others must do poorly</a>. And sometimes, you're the "other." </p><p><figure id="img-317351" class="figure image-figure-image  postload" data-type="img" data-e2e-name="image-figure-image" data-media-container="image" style=""><postload-img data-src="https://amp.businessinsider.com/images/541c483369beddc330834890-750-375.jpg" data-sizes="(min-width: 960px) and (max-width: 1259px) 640px, (min-width: 1260px) 960px, (-webkit-min-device-pixel-ratio: 2) 50vw, 100vw" data-srcset="https://amp.businessinsider.com/images/541c483369beddc330834890-160-80.jpg 160w, https://amp.businessinsider.com/images/541c483369beddc330834890-320-160.jpg 320w, https://amp.businessinsider.com/images/541c483369beddc330834890-480-240.jpg 480w, https://amp.businessinsider.com/images/541c483369beddc330834890-640-320.jpg 640w, https://amp.businessinsider.com/images/541c483369beddc330834890-750-375.jpg 750w, https://amp.businessinsider.com/images/541c483369beddc330834890-960-480.jpg 960w, https://amp.businessinsider.com/images/541c483369beddc330834890-1136-568.jpg 1136w, https://amp.businessinsider.com/images/541c483369beddc330834890-1334-667.jpg 1334w, https://amp.businessinsider.com/images/541c483369beddc330834890-1536-768.jpg 1536w, https://amp.businessinsider.com/images/541c483369beddc330834890-1920-960.jpg 1920w" data-alt="gambling table" ></postload-img><span class="image-source-caption"><figcaption class="image-caption" data-e2e-name="image-caption">Investing isn't just gambling.</figcaption><span class="image-source" data-e2e-name="image-source"><a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/15609463@N03/14215459553">Flickr / Jamie McCaffrey</a></span></span></figure></p><h3> About strategy </h3><p><strong>Starting early is a major advantage.</strong> In your 20s, your biggest asset is time. Even when you're just investing in retirement savings, nothing can make up for the <a href=http://www.businessinsider.com/compound-interest-retirement-funds-2014-3 > effect of compound interest</a>. Also, if you lose money in the market, you'll have more time to make it back before you need it.

It's a better bet to research the company and <a href=http://www.businessinsider.com/why-i-just-bought-1000-of-twitter-stock-2014-5 target=_blank>make your own decision</a> than to blindly jump on the stock of the moment. </p><p><strong>Your long-term strategy has nothing to do with that morning's news.</strong> Most investors shouldn't "buy" or "sell" every time it's recommended on TV. There's an <a href=http://www.businessinsider.com/mastering-passive-investing-movie-2013-3 > entire documentary</a> explaining why active investing &mdash; buying and selling stocks strategically and often &mdash; doesn't work for most people. </p><p><strong>Getting too attached to individual stocks can be dangerous.</strong> If you own a particular security you're attached to for sentimental reasons or because of its past performance, you might be reluctant to ditch it even if your advisor or investment professional says to. Securities are <a href=http://www.businessinsider.com/why-you-wont-ditch-underperforming-stock-2014-7 target=_blank>only as good as how they're performing currently</a>, and you have to be willing to let low performers go. </p><p><strong>You don't need to check constantly.</strong> If you've caught sight of a stock ticker (on <a href=http://www.businessinsider.com/ >Business Insider</a>, for example), you're probably aware that <a href=http://www.businessinsider.com/mastering-passive-investing-movie-2013-3 > markets go up and down</a> every day, and so do individual stocks. If you're investing for the long term and aren't an investing professional, you don't need the anxiety of a running ticker on your desktop. </p><p><strong>Don't invest money you'll need soon.</strong> If you'll need quick access to liquid cash in the short term, you won't want to park that money in the stock market. Some professionals say you shouldn't invest money you'll need in the next five years, because if the market goes down, you won't have enough time to recoup those funds. </p><p><figure id="img-841548" market.</strong><a href=http://www.businessinsider.com/theres-no-such-thing-as-a-normal-market-2014-9 target=_blank>They just can't</a>. While professionals can make educated guesses, predicting the market is predicting the future, and no one can do it.
