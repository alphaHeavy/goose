                        <h1 class="first-title first-title-adjustment">A second federal appeals court has ruled against Trump’s travel ban</h1>
                    <div class="grid-row">

                    <div class="first-post-content first-post-content-adjustment">
                        <div class="grid-row">
                            <p>The revised travel ban that President Trump ordered earlier this year, after his first one was blocked, has been ruled unconstitutional once again.                         <a class="article-link" href ="/can-the-ipad-pro-replace-a-laptop-wrong-question-4040552"data-event-category="User Action" data-event-action="Link Clicked: Title" data-event-label="“Can the iPad Pro replace a laptop?” Wrong question!">
                            <h2 class="first-title first-title-adjustment">“Can the iPad Pro replace a laptop?” Wrong question!</h2>
                        </a>
                    <div class="grid-row">
                        <div class="title-divider"></div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="first-post-content first-post-content-adjustment">
                        <div class="grid-row">
                            <p>I spent a good chunk of the past few days <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/40429655/10-5-inch-ipad-pro-review-a-better-window-into-the-world-of-apps">reviewing Apple&apos;s new 10.5-inch iPad Pro</a>. And really, I tried to judge whether it was a great iPad&#x2013;which it is, although it should be better still with the upcoming iOS 11&#x2013;rather than being fixated on whether it&apos;s something a typical person would buy to replace a laptop.</p>
<p>If you take the latter approach, as people often do when reviewing iPads, you&apos;re likely to conclude that the iPad Pro should have a mouse. And that the lack of laptop staples like full-blown Microsoft Office is a problem. And that the fact that Apple&apos;s Smart Keyboard doesn&apos;t feel much like a classic notebook keyboard might be a deal-breaker.</p>

                    <div class="grid-row">

                    <div class="first-post-content first-post-content-adjustment">
                        <div class="grid-row">
                            <p>New York&apos;s venerated theater organization, the Public Theater, is facing a PR disaster over its long-running free &quot;Shakespeare in the Park&quot; series. The organization is mounting a production of&#xA0;<i>Julius Caesar</i>, which (spoiler alert!) ends with the Roman politician being assassinated. Many far-right online personas&#x2014;including Donald Trump Jr.&#x2014;consider this to be offensive given that Caesar appears Trump-like in this particular production. (It depicts him with blonde hair and modern attire.)</p>
<p>A flurry of online trolling and hysteria over the show has resulted in <b>two corporate sponsors&#x2014;Bank of America and Delta&#x2014;<a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/06/11/arts/delta-airline-trump-public-theater-julius-caesar.html">cutting ties</a> with the Public Theater</b>.&#xA0;But not everyone is ready to cut and run. Today the <i>New York Times</i>, another corporate sponsor, confirmed to me that it will continue supporting the theater.&#xA0;</p>
<p>Here&apos;s the <i>Times&apos; </i>statement:</p>
<blockquote><p>&quot;We have sponsored Shakespeare in the Park for 20 years. As an institution that believes in free speech for the arts as well as the media, we support the right of the Public Theater to stage the production as they chose.&quot;&#xA0;</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Hopefully Delta and BofA will read it and learn something. This entire ordeal is sad and annoying because, one, free speech exists so that theater organizations can put on &quot;edgy&quot; shows, and, two, this show is not edgy&#x2014;it&apos;s a <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/person/william-shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> play where <i>everyone already knows the ending</i>. <a class="post-author author-initials author-initials-adjustment" data-event-category="User Action" data-event-action="Link Clicked: Author Byline" data-event-label="Author: Cale-Guthrie Weissman" href="https://www.fastcompany.com/user/cale-guthrie-weissman" target="_blank">CGW</a></p>

                            <h2 class="first-title first-title-adjustment">Big banks take on Apple Pay and Venmo with the launch of their own peer-to-peer network</h2>
                        </a>
                    <div class="grid-row">
                        <div class="title-divider"></div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="first-post-content first-post-content-adjustment">
                        <div class="grid-row">
                            <p><b><a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/company/venmo">Venmo</a> used to be revolutionary. </b>Now, the PayPal-owned mobile app is just one of many peer-to-peer payment options, alongside <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/company/apple">Apple</a> Pay, Facebook Messenger&#x2014;and Zelle.&#xA0;</p>
<p>Uh, Zelle? Yes, you read that right. In a rare bout of big bank collaboration, players including JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, and Wells Fargo <a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/us-usa-banks-payments-zelle-idUSKBN1931C2">teamed up</a> to enable instant money transfers. <b>Today they are going live, making Zelle available through the apps of more than 30 banking institutions. </b>If they succeed in winning converts, &quot;Send money with Zelle&quot; could become the new &quot;Venmo me.&quot;&#xA0;</p>
<p>Zelle arrives just in time; digital-first competitors are beginning to pose a real threat to banks&apos; relationship with customers. In addition, <b>banks expect Zelle to reduce the costs associated with managing cash and checks</b>.</p>
<p>So which service should you use? <a href="https://www.fastcodesign.com/90128304/the-subtle-psychology-of-apple-pay-and-how-it-could-cost-you">Over at <i>Co.Design</i></a>, <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/person/mark-wilson">Mark Wilson</a> highlights a key difference between Zelle and Apple Pay:&#xA0;While Zelle will transfer funds directly into your checking or savings account, Apple Pay, like Venmo, will keep funds within its own ecosystem unless you take further action.&#xA0;</p>

                            <h2 class="first-title first-title-adjustment">Uber shakeup: Here’s everything that leaked about Eric Holder’s investigation this weekend </h2>
                        </a>
                    <div class="grid-row">
                        <div class="title-divider"></div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="first-post-content first-post-content-adjustment">
                        <div class="grid-row">
                            <p><a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/company/uber">Uber</a>&apos;s board met for hours on Sunday to discuss and make a decision about the results of Eric Holder&apos;s investigation into the company&apos;s culture, according to the <i><a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/06/11/technology/uber-holder-report.html?smprod=nytcore-iphone&amp;smid=nytcore-iphone-share&amp;_r=0">New York Times</a></i>. Here&apos;s what we know so far:</p>
<p>&#x2022; <b>The report recommends</b> <b>axing <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/person/emil-michael">Emil Michael</a></b>, Uber SVP and crony to CEO Travis Kalanick. But the board hasn&apos;t made a final decision on whether to fire him. Michael has been involved in the mishandling of an Indian woman&apos;s medical records, was present at an outing to a South Korean escort bar with employees, and also once threatened to hire a team to dig up negative information on a journalist who had written unfavorable stories about Uber.</p>
<p>&#x2022; <b>Kalanick has suggested he take leave of absence</b>&#xA0;to recover from a tragic event that took his mother&apos;s life. The board is allowing Kalanick to make this decision on his own.&#xA0;</p>
<p>&#x2022; A board representative says that i<b>t will accept all the recommendations put forth in Holder&apos;s report</b>.&#xA0;</p>
<p>The report follows a <a href="https://www.fastcompany.com/40426426/uber-x-odus-all-the-execs-who-have-left-the-company-in-2017">series of departures</a>, including <a href="https://news.fastcompany.com/ubers-latest-firing-an-exec-who-obtained-a-rape-victims-medical-records-4040199">Eric Alexander</a>, who <b>obtained the medical records of an Indian woman who was raped by an Uber driver</b>. As Uber attempts to clean up its act, it&apos;s facing a major decision in Egypt. The government is looking to pass a law that would require Uber to share its live ride-hailing data, also known as &quot;heaven,&quot; according to the <i><a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/06/10/world/middleeast/egypt-uber-sisi-surveillance-repression-careem.html?_r=1">New York Times</a></i>. If parliament passes a law requiring companies doing business in the country to keep servers on Egyptian soil and share data with law enforcement, it may need to make a major decision about whether or not to stay in the country.</p>
<p>Stay tuned for lots more Uber news this week. &#xA0;</p>
