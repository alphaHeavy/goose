<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="274" data-total-count="274">RIO RANCHO, N.M. — The plan sounded great during the real estate boom: build a midsize arena, stuff it with sports, music acts and monster trucks and create a centerpiece for the new city center being developed on a dusty mesa here, 20 miles north of downtown Albuquerque.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="424" data-total-count="698">But trouble started almost from the day the doors of Santa Ana Star Center opened in 2006. <a href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/news/business/companies/global-entertainment-corporation/index.html?inline=nyt-org" title="More information about Global Entertainment" class="meta-org">Global Entertainment</a>, the company hired to build and manage the arena, failed to book enough events, and the minor league hockey team it recruited <a title="Albuquerque Journal article." href="http://www.abqjournal.com/sports/2009/07/03/scorps-dormant-for-next-season.html">folded</a>. Attendance was light because of high ticket prices and the arena’s remote location. Unrealistic sales targets and high turnover among the arena’s staff added to the problems.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="294" data-total-count="992">The arena, which Global Entertainment said would be profitable in a year, has lost so much money that Rio Rancho has had to spend millions of dollars each year to keep it afloat. The city fired Global Entertainment in 2009 and sued it to recoup hundreds of thousands of dollars in unpaid bills.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="261" data-total-count="1253">A new arena manager has brought in more business, but the losses have continued to mount, eating into the city’s already tight budget and pushing lawmakers to eliminate jobs and cut costs, including asking police officers to buy their own practice ammunition.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="390" data-total-count="1643">“If you look at the numbers that Global Entertainment presented to us, it was really, really questionable then, let alone during a recession,” said James C. Jimenez, the city manager in Rio Rancho, who was hired after the arena foundered. “If we didn’t have to allocate the money for debt service, our employees would have had raises and our budget would be in a better position.”</p><div id="story-ad-1" class="story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent">
    <div class="accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden">
    <p>Advertisement</p>
</div>

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="542" data-total-count="2185" id="story-continues-1">Rio Rancho is not alone in trying to salvage an arena opened by a growth-hungry town smitten with sports. Global Entertainment, based in Tempe, Ariz., persuaded nine other cities, including Youngstown, Ohio; Hidalgo, Tex.; and Wenatchee, Wash., that it could build them an arena, sell everything from tickets to hot dogs, and book enough events to turn a profit. Yet in most cases, Global failed to meet its financial projections and was replaced, leaving the cities to pay for expensive arenas that they often have had no experience running.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="356" data-total-count="2541">In many ways, the arenas were another creation — and victim — of the real estate bubble. But while bargain hunters buy foreclosed homes and governments support insolvent banks, there are few natural buyers for arenas that are unlikely to generate enough money to pay their operating costs, let alone the tens of millions of dollars spent to build them.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="210" data-total-count="2751">And although larger cities often have more wherewithal to absorb these kinds of long-term obligations, bedroom communities like Rio Rancho (population 87,000) have less leeway because they have smaller budgets.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="93" data-total-count="2844">So the towns are not just stuck with buyer’s remorse, but also growing financial headaches.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="425" data-total-count="3269">This, of course, was not anticipated when plans for Santa Ana Star Center were taking shape almost a decade ago. Rio Rancho was flush because of a boom in home building, a growing Intel chip factory and several new call centers. But because the city had too few stores, residents shopped in Albuquerque, leaving their retail dollars there, a problem in a state where sales taxes account for a large part of municipal revenue.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="276" data-total-count="3545">So Jim Owen, the mayor of Rio Rancho at the time, conceived of a new city center in a distant part of town away from Albuquerque. It would be anchored by city hall and the arena and would include new stores that would generate sales tax revenue that would be kept in the city.</p><figure id="media-100000000821223" class="media photo embedded layout-large-horizontal media-100000000821223 ratio-tall" data-media-action="modal" itemprop="associatedMedia" itemscope itemid="https://static01.nyt.com/images/2011/05/17/sports/17ranchopix2/17ranchopix2-jumbo.jpg" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject" aria-label="media" role="group">

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="24" data-total-count="3569"><strong> Coyotes, Not Businesses</strong></p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="314" data-total-count="3883">Yet the city center, with its windswept views of the Sandia <a href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/great-homes-and-destinations/lifestyles/mountains/index.html?inline=nyt-classifier" title="" class="meta-classifier">Mountains</a>, remains mostly a concept. Other than city hall and the arena, there are only a few buildings for offices and for the University of New Mexico West campus and Central New Mexico Community College. Coyotes routinely run through the parking lots.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="234" data-total-count="4117">The collapse of the real estate market undoubtedly helped stall the development. But Owen also blames his successor and developers for undoing his work. The arena, he said, remains a good idea in the context of the larger development.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="253" data-total-count="4370">“The arena was one piece to get the city center going,” he said. “We knew it was difficult to start with, and we knew we would subsidize it for a while. But a hundred years from now, people will look back and say, ‘Gee, this was planned.’ ”</p><div id="story-ad-2" class="story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent">
    <div class="accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden">

</div>

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="459" data-total-count="4829" id="story-continues-2">According to several former employees at Global Entertainment, however, Santa Ana Star Center was flawed from the outset. Like other arenas that Global built, Star Center, with 6,500 seats, was too small to attract big-name bands yet too big for up-and-coming acts, they say. The Star Center also competes with sites in Albuquerque, including theaters run by Native American tribes that often book music acts as loss leaders to draw gamblers to their casinos.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="312" data-total-count="5141">“Global’s thrust is the smaller markets and 6,000-seat buildings, which are a funny size for the touring industry,” said Doug Priestap, the first ticket manager at the arena in Rio Rancho. “Global got in way over its head. But these small towns, they get stars in their eyes and want to have an arena.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="393" data-total-count="5534">Global Entertainment also owns the <a title="League Web site." href="http://www.centralhockeyleague.com/">Central Hockey League</a> and put its teams in many of the arenas. But hockey has been a hard sell in places like Rio Rancho, where the Scorpions of the C.H.L. failed in 2009. Attendance to see N.B.A. Development League teams has been up and down. The owners of the Thunderbirds, who played in Rio Rancho this season, are reportedly seeking additional investors.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="22" data-total-count="5556"><strong> Quickly a Money Loser</strong></p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="307" data-total-count="5863">The arena was a money loser from the beginning. It lost $241,000 in its first nine months, ending in mid-2007, and millions of dollars since. Rio Rancho expects to pay $3.6 million this year to keep the arena running and to pay for its bonds, money that was supposed to be covered by profits from the arena.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="375" data-total-count="6238">The arena now consumes nearly 7 percent of the city’s $53.8 million budget, and workers have been furloughed, spending on parks has been curtailed and a reserve fund has been drawn down. In January, Jimenez, the city manager, asked for an additional $550,000 for arena upkeep and repairs, including refinishing the basketball court and buying new glass for the hockey rink.</p>        <a class="visually-hidden skip-to-text-link" href="#story-continues-3">Continue reading the main story</a>

    <div class="story-body">
        <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="310" data-total-count="6548" id="story-continues-4">The city is also suing Global Entertainment to recoup more than $300,000 that it claims the company owes dozens of local businesses that provided services at the arena. Gary Williams, who owns a video-production company in Albuquerque named in the suit, said he was owed $6,000 for work he did at hockey games.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="183" data-total-count="6731">“I gave up after a year of trying,” Williams said of his attempts to be paid. “I turned around and paid all my employees out of my pocket. It’s a live-and-learn experience.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="377" data-total-count="7108">Controversy is not new in Rio Rancho. The city became nationally known in the 1970s when four executives from the Amrep Corporation were convicted of mail and land fraud for their role selling patches of desert in Rio Rancho to residents of New York and elsewhere. Buyers accused Amrep of using high-pressure techniques to sell lots that, they learned, had little resale value.</p><figure id="media-100000000821847" class="media photo embedded layout-large-horizontal media-100000000821847 ratio-tall" data-media-action="modal" itemprop="associatedMedia" itemscope itemid="https://static01.nyt.com/images/2011/05/17/sports/jp-rancho-3/jp-rancho-3-jumbo.jpg" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject" aria-label="media" role="group">
    <span class="visually-hidden">Photo</span>
    <div class="image">
            <img src="https://static01.nyt.com/images/2011/05/17/sports/jp-rancho-3/jp-rancho-3-jumbo.jpg" alt="" class="media-viewer-candidate" data-mediaviewer-src="https://static01.nyt.com/images/2011/05/17/sports/jp-rancho-3/jp-rancho-3-jumbo.jpg" data-mediaviewer-caption="The N.B.A. Development League's New Mexico Thunderbirds have had uneven support at Santa Ana Star Center in Rio Rancho." data-mediaviewer-credit="Jake Schoellkopf/NBAE via Getty Images"  itemprop="url" itemid="https://static01.nyt.com/images/2011/05/17/sports/jp-rancho-3/jp-rancho-3-jumbo.jpg"/>

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="273" data-total-count="7381">For years, Rio Rancho residents, including many from the East Coast who wanted to live more cheaply and quietly, resisted incorporating the town. Rio Rancho was so off the beaten track that the F.B.I. reportedly located some members of its witness protection program there.</p><div id="story-ad-3" class="story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent">
    <div class="accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden">
    <p>Advertisement</p>
</div>
<a class="visually-hidden skip-to-text-link" href="#story-continues-5">Continue reading the main story</a>
</div>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="578" data-total-count="7959" id="story-continues-5">But as the city grew, its aspirations came to include an arena. The same was true of in Wenatchee, halfway between Seattle and Spokane, where Global Entertainment also built an arena. That arena, home to the Wenatchee Wild of the North American Hockey League and the Wenatchee Valley Venom of the Indoor Football League, has also failed to turn a profit, and roughly 15 percent of the city’s $22 million budget is devoted to paying off the bonds for Town Toyota Center. City jobs have been eliminated, overtime cut and street cleaning and hours at the public pool scaled back.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="179" data-total-count="8138">“We’re down to muscle and bone now,” said Jim Bailey, a city council member in Wenatchee. “Unless we can generate new income somewhere, it’s going to be layoffs next.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="20" data-total-count="8158"><strong> Problems With Bonds</strong></p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="359" data-total-count="8517">Complicating matters, the collapse of the financial markets in 2008 meant that bonds could not be issued to pay for the Wenatchee arena. So a three-year loan that comes due in December, with the city as guarantor, was taken out. The city is now racing to sell bonds before the loan expires but is having difficulty because the arena can barely cover expenses.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="111" data-total-count="8628">“If they can’t get bonds, that would mean default, and then everyone starts suing everyone,” Bailey said.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="448" data-total-count="9076">In Prescott Valley, Ariz., about 90 miles north of Phoenix, Global Entertainment built and still runs Tim’s Toyota Center. But several investors who bought some of the $35 million in bonds issued to pay for the arena have sued the company and its top executives, the city and its underwriter, alleging that they ignored pessimistic feasibility studies and knowingly issued overly optimistic growth forecasts so they could win higher bond ratings.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="472" data-total-count="9548">Forecasts in the bond prospectus estimated that 480,000 people a year would attend events at the arena, which opened in November 2006. Actual attendance was closer to 200,000. Teams that play there include the Arizona Sundogs of the C.H.L. and the Arizona Adrenaline of the Indoor Football League. Global Entertainment also scheduled about 70 events a year, half as many as projected. The finances were in such disarray that Fitch Ratings dropped its ratings on the bonds.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="24" data-total-count="9572"><strong> Developer Cites Economy</strong></p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="252" data-total-count="9824">Global Entertainment blames the recession for the troubles at the arenas it built, and the company more broadly. “We continue to be challenged significantly by the economy,” Richard Kozuback, its president, wrote in its most recent earnings filing.</p><div id="story-ad-4" class="story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent">
    <div class="accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden">
    <p>Advertisement</p>
</div>
<a class="visually-hidden skip-to-text-link" href="#story-continues-6">Continue reading the main story</a>
</div>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="297" data-total-count="10121" id="story-continues-6">Indeed, revenue fell 52 percent in the three months that ended Feb. 28, to $1.6 million, as Global exited the food-service business and arena-management fees dried up. The company said it had bought insurance for its executives to protect against any court claims, and had sought additional loans.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="114" data-total-count="10235">Global Entertainment’s stock price, which once traded as high as $9, is now worth as little as a nickel a share.</p><button class="button comments-button  theme-speech-bubble-large" data-skip-to-para-id="">
</button>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="222" data-total-count="10457">But for many of the cities that bought Global Entertainment’s sales pitch, the company’s fate is a footnote in their larger struggle to manage and pay for arenas that many people now think should never have been built.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="264" data-total-count="10721">“You sit back and wonder how they missed this,” Bailey, the Wenatchee councilman, said of his predecessors who approved the arena. “Even in good times, it wouldn’t have penciled out, and then we got hit by the perfect storm during the economic downturn.”</p><div id="addenda" class="addenda">
    <div class="story-addendum story-content theme-correction">
        <strong> Correction: May 17, 2011 </strong> <br/>
        <p>An earlier version of this article erroneously stated that Wells Fargo was the underwriter for $35 million in bonds issued to pay for Tim&#8217;s Toyota Center in&#160;Prescott Valley, Ariz. Wells Fargo is the trustee for the bondholders and initiated a lawsuit on their behalf.</p>    </div>
