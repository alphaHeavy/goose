							<div class="articleBodyText">
								<p>TOKYO -- Japan's augmented Global Positioning System is expected to create a host of new business opportunities, from highly sophisticated car navigation and drone delivery to autonomous driving of construction equipment.&nbsp;</p><div class="page_limit free"></div><div class="page_limit"></div><p>The second of a planned four satellites that will form the network was successfully launched Thursday from the Tanegashima Space Center.&nbsp;Information from the first Michibiki quasi-zenith satellite launched in September 2010 is already in use, and Japan plans to launch two more by March 2018.</p><p>The Global Positioning System, the network of global navigation satellites operated by the U.S., is widely used by car navigation systems and smartphones for positional information, but it is accurate only to 10 meters.</p><p>With the network of four Michibiki satellites, Japan will have a system that augments the data from existing GPS satellites to provide positional information with an accuracy of better than 1 meter, and in best-case situations an accuracy of 6cm. The goal is to have the network up and running next spring.</p><p>Such high-level accuracy will take car navigation to a new level. Not just the road but even the lane can be identified, providing the means for directing drivers more accurately in traffic. This kind of precise positional information will also prove useful for self-driving cars.</p><p>East Nippon Expressway (Nexco East) is looking into using the navigation satellites to optimize the routes of snowplows and drive accurately even during blizzards. The company plans to conduct road tests in Hokkaido this winter, aiming to develop a way for inexperienced snowplow drivers to work with the efficiency of a seasoned veteran.</p><p>The industrial machinery maker <a href="/Company/05HYZK-E" target="_self" alt="05HYZK-E" class="mwMoTrg">Hitachi Zosen</a> has developed a receiver for the radio signals from the Michibiki satellites that can be used to guide drones delivering packages to places like remote islands. It is also testing the use of the receivers to guide unmanned trucks between rice fields. New businesses can be developed in Australia and Southeast Asia, where the signals from the Michibiki satellites reach.</p><p>The Japanese construction giant <a href="/Company/05HWNR-E" target="_self" alt="05HWNR-E" class="mwMoTrg">Obayashi</a> has developed devices that use satellite signals so cranes can move around construction sites without touching power cables, and excavators can dig down to precise depths. The company hopes to reduce the number of human workers at its construction projects by boosting the precision of unmanned machinery.</p><p>(Nikkei)</p>
